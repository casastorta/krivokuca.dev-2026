It is 2020, and Netflix has during this summer released a German show about medical
research, ethics, crime and punishment (ok, maybe not the last one). And yet, unsurprisingly,
it's not about COVID (because it takes time to produce TV shows). Is it any good, though?
Well, it's kind of a mixed bag. Allow me to try and explain...

<!--more-->

![](/blog/images/biohackers-poster.jpg)

### The Good

Let me start with this: **I really like Biohackers**. But almost everything which is good
about it also is kinda bad. I've told you, it really is a mixed bag.

### Fast pace

Let's start from something which might actually convince you to watch it: **it is _really_
fast paced show**. But really. Almost to the fault (and more on that later). The plot
twists and turns in a moment. Whole season 1 has 6 episodes, and it could have easily been
a 10 episode season while still keeping faster pace than average TV show today. But it's not,
and I do like it. It's a refreshing change to see a really fast paced show after a decade or
two of TV shows which were introducing us in the plot slowly, working out characters for the
first season or two, focusing whole episodes on side quests of the main characters, to buy
time and I don't know what - advertiser credits?

### It's incredibly empowering show for female equality

While the show has problems with the depth of its characters (more on that later), Luna
Wedler and Jessica Schwarz pulled out great performance to the limits of the script
they've needed to follow.

Pitifully, the show doesn't let either of them perform like Diane Kruger did in "In
The Fade", which is one of the more spectacular female lead roles I have ever seen in a
TV show or a movie. But to the level of working with what they were given, both did a
great job and have overshadowed all of the other characters; specially the male ones.

### It's doing science reasonably (mostly)

You know which movie or TV show is a gold standard for computer hacking depiction in
popular culture? It's not super cringy "Hackers" from the 90s (as much as most of us who
grew up watching that movie love it). It's not "Matrix" either (oh hell no, that movie
gets so many things wrong on practical level). It's not even the super pretentious
"Antitrust" since it got so many practical things completely wrong (albeit not so subtle
Microsoft/Gates references worked more than ok). No, it's "Mr. Robot".

"Mr. Robot" does everything around computer hacking right, with a touch of fiction.
All the hacking tools characters in "Mr. Robot" use actually exist, and many of the
methodologies used to hack devices are correct, although they've been simplified
to work on the screen in your living room and not to bore the hell out of the
average viewer.

So, is "Biohackers" doing for biotech and medical science what "Mr. Robot" did for
computer hackers?

No, not really. It has gaps (again, more on that later). But it does some things well,
and I would argue - important things. All the buzzwords science geeks throw out in the
open function in the context and all of the laboratory equipment pretty much works as
shown (again, with a disclaimer that things are often sped up for the dramatization).
If you're watching this show not only for fun, but you want to learn something about
biotech and modern medicine; and you have no prior exposure to any of it; you could
just learn something new and potentially useful.

On top of that, it is a very up to date show. Again, to the fault - I yet have to see
a student of biology, chemistry, or a future MD who succesfully plays with CRISPR at
home to produce cheap variations of vegetarian food at home with no expensive
equipment. It simply doesn't work like that. But hey, a show which introduces CRISPR
to the general public and kinda touches on the basics of it is a scientific win in a
way. Maybe it inspires someone to pursue academic credentials in one of the fields.

## The bad

### Fast pace (bad sides)

As I've mentioned - Biohackers is a really fast paced show. Downside to this is that plot
is not horribly elaborate - for it to move fast there needed to be gaps, and nothing should
have been too complex to untie.

### Characters are kinda shallow

Another part of the price Biohackers pay to its ridiculously fast pace is shallow characters.
There is this old joke about Hollywood blockbuster saving on production costs by introducing a
disabled LGBTQ POC character which doesn't really fit in the narrative to save on casting and
staffing and avoid, joke claims, industry standard requirement of having at least one disabled,
one homosexual and one minority character.

I know it's a horrible joke, just bear with me here, please.

So, Biohackers is kind of the opposite. Characters, including main protagonists, are so shallow
that claiming how they are predictable is understatement. It doesn't matter if we talk of one
of the supporting characters or main female protagonists (who pulled their characters great to
the level script allowed them to do) - every action of every character is easily predictable.
If you miss correctly predicting what happens next, it's likely only due to fast pace of the show.
It's almost like the pace shortened the production time of the show so much that they've had
excess of funds and decided to make existing character more shallow by taking out some complex
traits of them and assigning them one by one each to supporting characters.

There is a geeky guy (ok, it's a show which happens at University, so everyone's a geek; but there
are characters in the show which go beyond the reasonable measure to be presented as "geeky"),
and there is another extremely geeky character but - female one. There is a mandatory promiscuous
rich party girl. Oh yeah but there is also a hot and sexy (and willing to cuddle) male character.

### It's an incredibly white show

Geeky girl does also play minority role - she is (of course, what else) Asian, while **all**
**other** **characters** **are** **white**. Let me repeat - pretty much everybody in the show
is white. Germany is for half a century now not as white as this show is presenting it. But,
also, it might not be far from the truth - University in Freiburg (where everything plays out)
could actually be not overly populated by the students of immigration background.


## The Ugly

### It does science badly (arguably)

Look, I know, I've praised this show above for getting the science right. But I did tell you it
has gaps, didn't I?

So here it is - when it does good to promote science, this show totally rocks. But when it fails,
it fails really hard.

Ok, a bit of a detour. Let me tell you a story.

Historically I have worked in biotech and big pharma. At one of these positions, I've worked with
a colleague who was Russian (not so relevant, but for the context), holder of a PhD in a computer
science from one of the good German universities and generally a person of many qualities - where
being a relaxed and funny person wasn't the one. I have really loved spending time and talking to
this colleague, but honestly - majority of people are probably more likeable to the general public
than this person.

One time we've talked about what in the popular culture influenced us. This colleague, you can
imagine, didn't have strong opinions (or did, but disliked it overall) on the popular music,
so we've ended up talking about movies and books. And both being nerds I thought it's a good
idea to talk about science-fiction references. Well, my colleague wasn't impressed - I was told
immediately - "you know, S in SF stands for science. So most of the SF is shitty, and only
good SF is the one which gets the science part right". So, very few things post-Asimov worked
for this colleague.

I've came to agree with that attitude though. I feel silly today calling Star Trek (for example)
**science** fiction - it's a pure fiction which leans on fantasies of the technological advantages
of the future. It's not that I don't like the whole franchise, but let's name things properly.
While Star Trek and shows alike inspired a lot of future engineers and scientists, honestly there
is very little to no science in most of the contemporary "SF" books, shows and movies.

And Biohackers try to do science right. Again, I did praise it for it. But then it gets some
things completely wrong, or it feel completely out of place how some scientific terms and
methodologies are overly simplified.

Does it make it unwatchable? For me not even close - I've enjoyed the whole show. But do not be
surprised if your scientific significant other cries in despair few times during this show and
holds you a lecture how this show should try and keep to the basics of science at minimum.
Because at times it basically fails to do so.

### Things **really** don't work like that

For this one I am still not sure if it's a price being paid to the fast pace of the show
or Christian Ditter and the rest of the production crew really don't understand how much
regulation affects scientific research.

Some things in the show simply don't work like that. I mean, ok, can the big shot head of the
private company run incognito black laboratory in her basement? I mean, sure, that's possible.
Not extremely likely, but possible I guess, yeah.

Ok, but can she pull out experiments there which she would not be able to in her own company's
state of the art lab? That's where it gets beyond stretched.

Ok, but it is valid idea to have off-site archive of the sensitive patient data stored in the
home of the owner of the company? Like fucking absolutely no way. It doesn't work like that
even remotely. Yeah sure, people could one way or the other take out data and store it at home,
that is always possible. But everybody knowing this and nobody raising suspicion that it is
totally illegal, specially in Germany? Again, no way. It simply doesn't work like that, none
of it.

Do students of the natural sciences produce genetically modified mice which glow in the dark
to carry them around? Again, sure, kinda possible, but I would assume they are too busy to
pull that shit up (even when they are not assistants to the big shot head of biotech company
outside of Uni). But it's, um, highly unlikely.

Ok, but students genetically modifying weed to be flourescent so their hands and faces glow
in the dark while they smoke pot? As much as I appreciate this show trying to present
nerdy students as cool gang, this is taking it a bit too far. Again, none of it works like
that.

Nothing tied to what people do people in biotech do professionally or in their spare time
at all works like the shows presents it. This is one big facepalm which almost ruins the
show - for those who are even remotely aware of the regulatory framework medical research
and biotech has to work with.

But I guess it's fine - because it doesn't ruin it for the general public.

Still, risking to sound like a boring and not at all fun person and a party breaker - I will
say that a show based on biotech and medical research should at least acknowledge the
regulatory framework and revolve plot around it - not simply crashing
head-first against it and ignoring that it exists.

## Conclusion

So, what gives?

Well, I really want you to watch "Biohackers". The show is good enough, it's fast paced so it
will not require you to spend days of your life to watch it. And it's a fun show. Also, end
of season 1 is a cliffhanger and I want you to help Netflix to decide that it deserves second
season.

There are so many so much worse shows on Netflix and other streaming services, and honestly -
not so much better ones.

As the old internet joke goes - it's a 5/7 show, would watch it again.
