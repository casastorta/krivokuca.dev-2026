Kubernetes Is Not a Deploy Button

January 29, 2026 · 2 min read

There is a pattern I’ve seen at every company past a certain size. Someone reads a blog post, attends a KubeCon talk, or hears a competitor mention Kubernetes in a press release. A decision is made. Six months later, a team of four is maintaining a 47-node cluster to serve an application that previously ran fine on three EC2 instances.

This is not a Kubernetes problem. It’s a complexity budget problem.

What Kubernetes actually is

Kubernetes is a distributed systems operating environment. It gives you primitives for scheduling containers, managing configuration, handling network traffic, and expressing desired state. What it does not give you is simplicity. It trades operational simplicity for operational power and consistency at scale.

The hidden costs

  • Cognitive load. Your engineers need to understand pods, deployments, services, ingresses, RBAC, namespaces, resource limits, and PodDisruptionBudgets before they can do anything meaningful.
  • Operational surface area. Control plane upgrades, etcd backups, node pool management, certificate rotation.
  • Debugging complexity. A request fails. Is it the app? The service mesh? The ingress controller? The network policy? DNS?

When it’s absolutely worth it

When you have multiple teams deploying multiple services, when you need consistent environments across dev, staging, and production, when you’re running workloads that genuinely need horizontal scaling and self-healing — Kubernetes earns its complexity tax.

The mistake isn’t adopting Kubernetes. The mistake is adopting it before you’ve outgrown what came before.
