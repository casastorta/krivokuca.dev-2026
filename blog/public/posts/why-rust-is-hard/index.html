Why Rust Is Hard (And Why That's the Point)

February 18, 2026 · 2 min read

The first time the Rust compiler rejected my code, I thought it was broken. The second time, I thought I was broken. By the hundredth time, I started to understand that the compiler was right all along — I just hadn’t been thinking carefully enough about ownership.

The central insight is this: every bug the borrow checker prevents is a bug that would have shipped in C, C++, or even Go. Memory safety violations, data races, use-after-free — Rust makes these impossible at compile time rather than painful at 3am.

The honest answer is: it depends on your background. If you’ve spent years writing Python or JavaScript, the ownership model will feel alien. If you’ve survived manual memory management in C, you’ll recognise the problems Rust is solving — you’ve just been solving them manually, with varying degrees of success.

The compiler is not your enemy. It’s the world’s most patient code reviewer who never gets tired, never misses a case, and never lets things slide because it’s Friday afternoon.
